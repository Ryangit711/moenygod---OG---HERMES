# Money as Debt: The Modern System

## How Modern Money Actually Works

Most people think money works like this: you work, you get paid, you deposit it in a bank, the bank lends your deposit to someone else. **Wrong.**

Here's how it actually works: when a bank issues a loan, it doesn't lend out someone else's deposit. It **creates new money by typing a number into an account**. That number is debt. **Over 90% of money in circulation today is created this way — as bank loans.**

---

## The Bank Money Creation Process

1. A borrower walks into a bank and asks for a loan
2. The bank checks their creditworthiness
3. If approved, the bank **creates a deposit** in the borrower's account out of nothing
4. The borrower now has "money" to spend
5. That money didn't exist before the loan was made
6. The loan is an **asset** on the bank's balance sheet; the deposit is a **liability**
7. The borrower must repay the principal **plus interest**

The key insight: **the interest was never created.** Only the principal is created by the loan. So where does the money to pay interest come from? From someone else's loan. Which means the system requires **constant growth in debt** just to function.

---

## Debt in Modern Economies

Today **most money is debt**:

| Form | How It Works |
|------|--------------|
| **Bank deposits** | When you deposit cash, the bank records a liability ("we owe you X"). That liability is used for loans, payments, etc. |
| **Government bonds** | Sovereign debt is a promise to repay with interest; these bonds are traded like assets |
| **Credit cards & loans** | Direct consumer debt, recorded electronically |

The monetary system is essentially a web of promises — **IOUs** — that circulate as "money."

---

## Why the System Requires Growth

Because interest must be paid on every loan, but only principal is created:

- Total debt in the system = sum of all principals + sum of all interest owed
- Total money in the system = sum of all principals only
- The gap = interest that must be earned by competing for the existing money supply
- This means someone, somewhere, always defaults — or new loans must be issued to keep the system liquid

This is why there **must** be constant economic growth in a debt-based monetary system. Not because growth is inherently good. Because without it, the math collapses. Defaults cascade. Banks fail. Recessions and depressions follow.

---

## Inflation as Hidden Tax

Inflation is not just "prices going up." It is a **hidden transfer of wealth**:

- From **savers** (whose money loses purchasing power) → to **debtors** (whose debts shrink in real terms)
- The biggest debtor in any country = **the government**
- When the government prints money or the central bank expands the money supply, it is effectively taxing savers to pay down its own real debt
- This is why inflation is called "the invisible tax"

---

## Who Benefits?

| Who | How They Benefit |
|-----|-------------------|
| Central banks | Control the money supply; their decisions determine who wins and who loses |
| Commercial banks | Create money ex nihilo and charge interest on it — the most profitable business model ever devised |
| Large debtors (governments, corporations) | Their real debt burden shrinks with inflation |
| Asset holders (real estate, stocks) | Asset prices rise with monetary expansion |

| Who | How They Lose |
|-----|----------------|
| Wage earners | Wages lag behind inflation |
| Savers | Purchasing power erodes |
| Small debtors | Often crushed by interest they can never repay |
| The poor | No assets to hedge against inflation |

## The Fundamental Design

Money and debt are **engineered systems of obligation**:

- **Money** = a token of claim on other people's labor and resources
- **Debt** = a promise enforced by power (legal, military, social)
- Both exist because humans needed to track obligations at scale
- They were **never neutral** — from Sumer and Babylon to Wall Street, they are tools of power
- The question is always: **who creates the obligations, and who is forced to fulfill them?**
