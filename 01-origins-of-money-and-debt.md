# Origins of Money and Debt

## Why Money and Debt Are Not "Natural"

Air, water, oxygen — these exist whether humans are here or not. They are products of physics, chemistry, and biology. No one had to invent them. No one had to agree on them.

Money and debt are different. They **do not exist in nature**. No animal uses currency. No river pays interest. They are **purely social constructs** — they exist only because humans collectively agree to believe in them and enforce that belief through law, violence, and habit.

---

## The Barter Myth

Modern economics teaches that early humans bartered ("I'll give you 3 chickens for your goat") and then found it inconvenient, so they invented money. **This is false.**

Anthropologists like David Graeber (*Debt: The First 5000 Years*) showed that no known society primarily used barter before money. Barter only happens between strangers who don't trust each other — which is exactly the situation modern economists imagined as the "natural state" of humans.

---

## The Real Origin: Community Credit

Before money existed, humans lived in small communities. Within a village or tribe, people didn't barter — they **shared and tracked obligations**. If your neighbor helped you build a house, you owed them. Not in coins. In **social debt**. Everyone kept a mental ledger: who helped whom, who owed what.

This is the primal origin of both debt and money — **a memory of obligation**.

### Debt and Sin Share the Same Root Word

| Language | Word | Meaning |
|----------|------|---------|
| Sanskrit | *rṇa* | both "debt" and "guilt/sin" |
| Latin | *debitum* | "that which is owed" → English "debt" and "debit" |
| Aramaic | *hob* | both "debt" and "sin" |

Jesus uses this double meaning in the Lord's Prayer — "forgive us our debts" / "forgive us our trespasses."

Debt was originally **moral**. It was about human relationships, reciprocity, and obligation within a community. Not numbers on a screen.

## How Debt Became Money: The Four Steps

### Step 1 — Social Credit

I help you, you owe me. Tracked in memory. Interest = zero. This is still how families and close communities work.

### Step 2 — Temples and Rulers

When societies grew larger (Mesopotamia, ~3000 BC), temples became the central storehouses — grain, livestock, tools. People deposited surplus there. The temple gave them a **receipt** (clay tablet). Those receipts could be transferred to others. **That receipt was money.** And the record of who owed what to the temple was **debt**.

### Step 3 — The State Weaponizes It

Rulers realized something powerful: if you demand that people **pay taxes in a specific currency that only you issue**, you force everyone to use your money. You create demand by threat. This is how fiat money actually works — not by trust, but by the state saying "pay me in this or I'll punish you." Military, courts, police enforce it.

### Step 4 — Banks Create Money from Nothing

When a bank issues a loan, it doesn't lend out someone else's deposit. It creates new money by typing a number into an account. That number is debt. **Over 90% of money in circulation today is created this way — as bank loans.** No bank, no money. No debt, no money. They are the same thing now.

## The Historical Arc: Commodity Money → Coinage → Fiat

### Commodity Money

Objects that already had intrinsic value and were widely accepted:

| Commodity | Why It Worked |
|-----------|---------------|
| Shells (e.g., cowries) | Hard to counterfeit, easy to count, portable |
| Precious metals (gold, silver) | Durable, divisible (by melting or cutting), universally desired |
| Livestock, grain, salt | Essential for survival, thus inherently valuable |

These served two roles:
1. **Unit of account** — a common measure to price other goods
2. **Store of value** — could be saved and later used for transactions

### Coinage (~600 BC)

The Lydians (modern-day Turkey) minted the first standardized metal coins:

| Motivation | Effect |
|------------|--------|
| Standardization — fixed weight & purity | Eliminated need to weigh and assay each transaction |
| State authority — a ruler's "seal" guaranteed value | Built trust in the currency; allowed state to collect taxes in coin |
| Portability — small, uniform pieces | Made everyday trade far easier |

### Fiat Money

As economies grew, carrying large amounts of metal became impractical:

| Stage | Characteristics |
|-------|------------------|
| **Representative** | Paper notes redeemable for gold/silver |
| **Fiat** | Paper (or digital) money that the state declares legal tender, backed only by trust in the issuing authority |

Why societies accept fiat money despite no intrinsic value:
- **Legal enforcement** — taxes must be paid in the official currency
- **Network effects** — the more people accept it, the more useful it becomes
- **Institutional trust** — central banks manage supply, aiming for stability

---

## The Bottom Line

- **Money** = a token of claim on other people's labor and resources
- **Debt** = a promise enforced by power (legal, military, social)
- Both exist because humans needed to **track obligations at scale**, beyond what memory and trust alone could handle
- They were **never neutral** — from the very beginning in Sumer and Babylon, they were tools of power, used by temples, kings, and later banks to organize society in ways that benefited those at the top

The real question: **who gets to create the obligations, and who is forced to fulfill them?**
